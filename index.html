Why stoichiometry?

Stoichiometry exists because you cannot count molecules by looking at them or weighing them. This section explains why mass alone is insufficient — and what we do about it.

The fundamental problem with mass

In chemistry, reactions happen between molecules — not grams. Two completely different substances can weigh exactly the same but contain wildly different numbers of molecules. Reactions require molecules to meet in specific ratios, so you need the molecule count, not just the mass.

Real example from the packet
Adding 2 teaspoons of KOH to HCl gives more mass of product (KCl) than adding 2 teaspoons of LiOH — but the same number of molecules reacted in both cases. The mass difference comes from potassium being heavier than lithium. The chemistry is identical; only the mass differs.
Another example: silver nitrate + sodium chloride
If you mix 4 g AgNO₃ + 4 g NaCl, you do NOT necessarily get 4 g of each product. Every substance has its own molar mass, so 4 g of one substance contains a completely different number of molecules than 4 g of another. Weighing tells you the mass; only stoichiometry tells you how many molecules.

This is why "looks can be deceiving" is a theme of the class. A scale tells you mass — it does not tell you molecule count. Stoichiometry is the system chemists built to count molecules indirectly, using mass as a starting point only.

What stoichiometry actually is

Stoichiometry is the practice of quantifying a chemical reaction — finding out how many grams, molecules, or moles of something are involved. It lets you predict product amounts before running an experiment.

The burger analogy
If a burger requires 2 buns, 1 patty, and 1 ketchup packet, and you have 12 buns, 4 patties, and 5 ketchup packets, you can calculate exactly how many burgers you can make — and which ingredient runs out first (the patties). Chemistry works the same way; stoichiometry is just the tool for doing the math.

Stoichiometry specifically lets you find the grams, molecules, and/or moles of a substance in a reaction. If you know one of those three figures for any substance, you can calculate the other two for any other substance in the same equation.

Mass is the beginning, not the end

You start by weighing — it's the most accessible precision tool in a lab. But mass alone cannot tell you how many molecules you have, because every substance has a different "weight per molecule." Lavoisier confirmed the law of conservation of mass by weighing reactants and products — but as the packet notes, mass is only the beginning. You must follow it up with stoichiometry.

Why mass isn't enough — the HCl example
Add 2 teaspoons of KOH to HCl → get KCl (more mass than the base).
Add 2 teaspoons of LiOH to HCl → get LiCl (less mass than the base).
Same number of molecules reacted in both cases. If Sample A has more mass than Sample B, that does not mean Sample A has more molecules.

Four habits that prevent struggling

  • Always write units next to every number — a bare number is meaningless and makes errors invisible
  • Show all your work — if you make a mistake, there must be a written trail to find it
  • Solve problems exactly as taught, not in your own invented method — if you can only solve it your own way, there are concepts you're missing
  • Avoid shortcuts until you fully understand the underlying idea — shortcuts are for people who already understand everything perfectly

Significant figures

Sig figs communicate how precisely something was measured. They are not about rounding for convenience — they record the actual precision of your instrument and propagate that precision through calculations.

What sig figs represent

When you record a measurement as "1 meter" vs "1.00 meters," those communicate different precision. The first says you measured to the nearest meter. The second says you measured to the nearest centimeter. Sig figs encode that difference into the number itself.

The rule: start counting from the left at the first non-zero digit, and count every digit from there — with one important exception for trailing zeros.

The four counting rules

  • Non-zero digits (1–9) are always significant, no exceptions
  • Zeros sandwiched between non-zero digits are always significant (e.g. 5004 — all four digits count)
  • Leading zeros — zeros to the left of the first non-zero digit — are never significant (in 0.0042, only 4 and 2 count)
  • Trailing zeros — zeros at the end — are significant only if a decimal point is present somewhere in the number
The trailing zero rule — most commonly missed
1000 has 1 sig fig. 1000. has 4 sig figs. 1000.00 has 6 sig figs. The decimal point is the signal. Without it, trailing zeros are just placeholders and don't count.

Calculation rules

Multiplication and division: your answer has the same number of sig figs as the input with the fewest sig figs.

Example
4.5 × 6.75 = 30.375 → rounds to 30 (4.5 has only 2 sig figs, so the answer gets 2)

Addition and subtraction: your answer is limited to the least precise decimal place — not the fewest sig figs. Find which number is only reliable to the fewest decimal places, and round your answer to that same place.

Example
23,800 − 6,866 = 16,934 → rounds to 16,900 (23,800 is only reliable to the hundreds place)
Never round intermediate answers
Only round your final result. Rounding early compounds error through every subsequent calculation step. Carry full precision from your calculator until the very last step.

Special rounding rule for exactly 5: when the digit after your last sig fig is exactly 5 with nothing (or only zeros) after it, round to the even number. So 32.65 → 32.6 (6 is even), and 79.35 → 79.4 (4 is even). This prevents systematic bias from always rounding up.

Scientific notation

A compact system for writing very large or very small numbers. In chemistry you constantly encounter numbers like Avogadro's number (6.022 × 10²³) or atomic masses at the gram scale — scientific notation makes these manageable.

The format

Scientific notation always looks like: a number between 1 and 10, multiplied by a power of 10. The exponent tells you how far and which direction to move the decimal.

6.022 × 10²³ → decimal moves 23 places right (large number)

4.7 × 10⁻⁸ → decimal moves 8 places left (tiny number)

Converting to scientific notation

  1. Move the decimal until you have a number between 1 and 10. Count how many places you moved.
  2. Moved left (large number) → positive exponent. Moved right (small number) → negative exponent.
  3. Write as (number between 1 and 10) × 10^(exponent). Keep all sig figs from the original number.
Examples
340,000,000 → move decimal 8 places left → 3.4 × 10⁸
0.000000047 → move decimal 8 places right → 4.7 × 10⁻⁸
5.990 × 10⁻³ in long form → move decimal 3 places left → 0.005990 (keep trailing zero — it's significant)

Calculating with scientific notation

Handle numbers and powers of 10 separately, then combine. For multiplication: multiply the numbers and add the exponents. For division: divide the numbers and subtract the exponents.

Multiplication
(4 × 10⁻²)(1.5 × 10⁹) → 4 × 1.5 = 6 and −2 + 9 = 7 → 6 × 10⁷
Division
(8.0 × 10³) ÷ (4.0 × 10⁻⁷) → 8.0 ÷ 4.0 = 2.0 and 3 − (−7) = 10 → 2.0 × 10¹⁰
Squaring/cubing
(2.00 × 10⁻⁴)² → (2.00)² = 4.00 and (10⁻⁴)² = 10⁻⁸ → 4.00 × 10⁻⁸
Always check your answer
After calculating, verify the number out front is still between 1 and 10. If you get 35.6 × 10³, you must adjust it: move the decimal one place left and add 1 to the exponent → 3.56 × 10⁴.

Ratios & fractions

The same relationship between two numbers can be expressed in four equivalent ways. Stoichiometry uses all four depending on context — understanding how to move between them fluently is essential.

Four representations of the same relationship

FormWhat it meansExample: 6 blue out of 9 total
Divisionthe operation itself6/9
Fraction (simplified)part/whole2/3
Ratiopart:part comparison2:1 (blue:non-blue)
Decimaldivision carried out0.667
Percentdecimal × 10066.7%
Ratio vs fraction — a critical distinction
A ratio (2:1) compares part to part. A fraction (2/3) compares part to whole. For 6 blue and 3 non-blue socks: the ratio blue:non-blue is 2:1, but the fraction of the total that is blue is 2/3. These are different statements.
Why ratios matter in chemistry
Mole ratios from balanced equations are part:part comparisons — they don't involve the "whole." The balanced equation 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O tells you the ratio 2 mol H₂ : 1 mol O₂, not a fraction of a total.

Negative exponents and unit notation

A negative exponent on a unit means that unit is in the denominator. A slash and a negative exponent are two notations for exactly the same thing — your teacher may use either interchangeably.

10 kg × m⁻² is the same as 10 kg/m²

25 m × s⁻² is the same as 25 m/s²

3.5 moles/L is the same as 3.5 moles × L⁻¹

The mole

The mole is the bridge between the invisible world of atoms and the measurable world of grams. Everything in stoichiometry passes through it — understanding it thoroughly is non-negotiable.

What a mole actually is

A mole is a counting unit — exactly like a dozen (12) or a gross (144), except the number is 6.022 × 10²³. It was chosen so that the atomic mass of any element in amu numerically equals its molar mass in grams. Carbon's atomic mass is 12 amu, so 1 mole of carbon atoms weighs exactly 12 grams. This is not a coincidence — it's the definition.

Don't try to visualize the number
6.022 × 10²³ is incomprehensibly large — there's no useful way to "feel" it. Treat it as a conversion factor the same way you'd use 12 to convert dozens to individual items. The number's only job is to make atomic masses in amu equal molar masses in grams.

Three things one mole always equals

EqualityWhen it appliesUsed for
1 mol = 6.022 × 10²³ particlesalways, any substancemoles ↔ molecules/atoms
1 mol = molar mass in gramsalways — look up periodic tablemoles ↔ grams
1 mol = 22.4 L at STPgases only at standard T and Pmoles ↔ volume (gas)
Applied examples
1 mol Fe = 6.022 × 10²³ atoms Fe = 55.9 g Fe
1 mol O₂ = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules O₂ = 32 g O₂ = 22.4 L at STP
2 mol O₂ = 2 × 6.022 × 10²³ molecules O₂ = 64 g O₂ = 44.8 L at STP
Moles ≠ molecules — never confuse these
These words look similar and are related but mean completely different things. 1 mole contains 6.022 × 10²³ molecules. Never abbreviate "molecules" as "moles" in your work — they will be marked wrong.

Atomic weight vs atomic mass vs molar mass

TermDefinitionWhere foundExample
Atomic weightaverage mass of one atom averaged across all natural isotopesperiodic tableC = 12.011
Atomic massmass of one specific isotopegiven in a problemC-12 = exactly 12
Molar massmass of one mole of a molecule; add atomic weights togethercalculatedCO₂ = 44 g/mol

Your teacher often uses "atomic mass" for all three — that is fine for calculations. The distinction matters mainly in very high-precision contexts. Carbon's periodic table value is 12.011 (not exactly 12) because C-13 exists in nature and affects the average.

Avogadro's contribution

Avogadro's Law states that equal volumes of different gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules. This was important because it gave early chemists a way to compare molecule counts indirectly using volume — before Avogadro's number was formally defined. Unfortunately it only works for gases, but it gave birth to the idea of counting molecules by quantity rather than by mass alone.

6.022 × 10²³ is called Avogadro's number because his work on gases is what began the search for such a number — though he didn't calculate it himself.

Calculating molar mass

Molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance in grams/mol. You find it by adding up the atomic weights of every atom in the formula, multiplied by how many of each appear.

The process — step by step

  1. Write out the molecular formula and identify every element present
  2. Count how many atoms of each element appear — pay close attention to subscripts and parentheses
  3. Look up the atomic weight of each element on the periodic table (use one decimal place for this class)
  4. Multiply: (number of atoms) × (atomic weight) for each element
  5. Add all results — this is your molar mass in g/mol
Example: H₃PO₄
H: 3 × 1.0 = 3.0  |  P: 1 × 31.0 = 31.0  |  O: 4 × 16.0 = 64.0
Total = 98.0 g/mol

Parentheses — the most common error source

When a subscript appears outside parentheses, it multiplies every element inside. Students very commonly forget to distribute it to every element, not just the last one.

Example: (NH₄)₂SO₄
The 2 outside multiplies everything inside the parentheses:
N: 1 × 2 = 2 atoms → 2 × 14.0 = 28.0
H: 4 × 2 = 8 atoms → 8 × 1.0 = 8.0
S: 1 × 32.1 = 32.1  |  O: 4 × 16.0 = 64.0
Total = 132.1 g/mol
The most common mistake
Students read (NO₃)₂ and count 2 nitrogen and 2 oxygen. Correct count: 2 nitrogen and 6 oxygen. The subscript 2 multiplies both the N and the O₃ inside the parentheses.

Use one decimal place for atomic masses (Cl = 35.5, Fe = 55.9) unless your teacher specifies otherwise. This matches the worked examples in the packet. For elements where the decimal is negligible (Ag ≈ 108, Na ≈ 23), whole numbers are acceptable. When in doubt, match what your teacher uses in class examples.

Dimensional analysis

The single most important procedural skill in this unit. Every stoichiometry calculation uses it. Master this and the rest of the unit becomes a matter of plugging in the right numbers.

What it is and why it works

Dimensional analysis converts between units by multiplying by conversion factors — fractions that equal exactly 1. Because multiplying by 1 doesn't change a value, you can change units freely without changing what the number means. Units cancel just like numbers in fractions.

The template
Known × (conversion 1) × (conversion 2) × ... = Desired

Unit A × (Unit B / Unit A) × (Unit C / Unit B) = Unit C

Any unit appearing in both a numerator and denominator cancels out.

Equivalence statements and conversion factors

An equivalence statement says two different-looking things are equal (1 min = 60 sec). Every equivalence statement gives exactly two conversion factors — one for each direction of conversion.

From "1 minute = 60 seconds"
Factor 1: (1 min / 60 sec) → use to convert seconds to minutes (put sec in denominator)
Factor 2: (60 sec / 1 min) → use to convert minutes to seconds (put min in denominator)

Choose whichever puts the unit you want to cancel in the denominator.
If your units don't cancel — the conversion factor is flipped
This is the built-in error check. If your answer has wrong units, find the conversion factor with that unit and invert it. The math is right when the units work out.

Multi-step example (non-chemistry)

Drive 2778 miles at 70 mph, 6 hours/day. How many days?
Equivalences
70 miles = 1 hour  |  6 hours = 1 day
Setup
2778 miles × (1 hr / 70 miles) × (1 day / 6 hrs)
Result
= 2778 ÷ 70 ÷ 6 = 6.6 days

Chemistry example — grams to molecules

How many molecules in 3 g NaCl? (NaCl = 58.5 g/mol)
Equivalences
1 mol NaCl = 58.5 g  |  1 mol = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules
Setup
3 g NaCl × (1 mol NaCl / 58.5 g NaCl) × (6.022×10²³ molecules / 1 mol)
Answer
3.09 × 10²² molecules NaCl
The Flight 143 lesson
In 1983, an Air Canada 767 ran out of fuel mid-flight because ground crew used lb/L instead of kg/L for fuel density. Had they written units and checked cancellation, multiplying liters × lb/L gives pounds, not kilograms — an immediately obvious error. Always write your units.

What stoichiometry does

Stoichiometry is the bridge from what you can measure in a lab (grams) to what actually drives chemistry (molecule counts and ratios). This section explains the big picture before the details.

The central problem

Given grams of reactant A, how many grams of product B will form? You cannot answer this by just weighing things — different substances have different molar masses, so grams don't translate directly between substances. The only unit that lets you compare substances using the fixed ratio from a balanced equation is moles.

Typical stoichiometry question
You mix 5 grams of AgNO₃ with excess Na₂CO₃. How many grams of Ag₂CO₃ will form? This is completely unanswerable by just weighing — you need to know how many moles of AgNO₃ you have, what the mole ratio to Ag₂CO₃ is, and what Ag₂CO₃ weighs per mole.

The three steps — always in this exact order

  1. Step A — Balance the equation. The mole ratios come from the coefficients. If the equation isn't balanced, the coefficients are wrong and every number downstream will be wrong.
  2. Step B — Extract the mole ratio. Read the coefficients of your balanced equation to find the mole relationship between your known and desired substance.
  3. Step C — Set up dimensional analysis. Follow the molar map: grams A → moles A → moles B → grams B. Three conversion factors, every time.
These steps cannot be reordered
Skipping step A means your mole ratios are wrong. Using mole ratios before dimensional analysis leads to unit confusion. The order exists for reasons, not convention.

The molar map pathway

Mass-to-mass — the most complete type
grams A → moles A → moles B → grams B

Conversion factor 1: molar mass of A (grams A → moles A)
Conversion factor 2: mole ratio from balanced equation (moles A → moles B)
Conversion factor 3: molar mass of B (moles B → grams B)
Why can't you go grams A → grams B directly?
It would require a different specific conversion factor for every possible pair of substances — a database of thousands of values. Going through moles works universally because the mole ratio handles the substance-specific step, and molar masses handle the grams-to-moles steps.

Balancing equations

A balanced equation has the same number of each atom on both sides. This is required by the law of conservation of mass and is the mandatory first step before any stoichiometry calculation.

The one absolute rule

You may only change coefficients — the large numbers written in front of a formula. You must never change subscripts inside a formula. Changing H₂O to H₃O changes the substance entirely. Only the coefficient in front can be adjusted to balance.

Coefficients vs subscripts
Coefficients: how many molecules/moles of a substance. Subscripts: what the molecule is made of. Changing subscripts changes the chemistry — that is not balancing, it is inventing a different reaction.

The balancing process

  1. Write the unbalanced equation. Verify every formula is written correctly first — wrong formulas produce wrong products.
  2. Make a tally: count each atom type on the left and right separately.
  3. Start with the most complex molecule. Add coefficients to balance one element at a time.
  4. Balance metals first, then non-metals, then hydrogen, then oxygen last.
  5. Recount everything — both sides must match completely before proceeding.
Example: Al + HCl → AlCl₃ + H₂
Start: 1 Al, 1 H, 1 Cl left | 1 Al, 3 Cl, 2 H right
Need 3 Cl on left → 3HCl → now 3 H on left
3 H left vs 2 H right — need even numbers. Multiply H₂ side: try 3H₂ → need 6H left → 6HCl → need 2Al left
2Al + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂ ✓
Check: 2 Al, 6 H, 6 Cl each side ✓

Fractional coefficients

Sometimes you get a fraction (e.g. 3/2 O₂) as an intermediate step. This is valid mathematically but unconventional. Multiply every coefficient in the entire equation by the denominator to restore whole numbers.

Example: Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃
Need 2 Fe for Fe₂O₃, need 3 O so 3/2 O₂
Fractional: 2Fe + 3/2 O₂ → Fe₂O₃ (not ideal)
Multiply everything by 2: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ ✓

Mole ratios

Mole ratios are conversion factors extracted from the coefficients of a balanced equation. They are the bridge between substances — the step that makes this chemistry rather than just unit conversion.

Where mole ratios come from

Coefficients in a balanced equation tell you exactly how many moles of each substance react or are produced. Because the equation is balanced, these ratios are fixed by the law of conservation of mass. Any pair of substances in the equation gives one mole ratio.

From: 4FeS + 7O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ + 4SO₂
4 mol FeS : 7 mol O₂ : 2 mol Fe₂O₃ : 4 mol SO₂

Every pair gives a ratio:
4 mol FeS = 7 mol O₂  |  4 mol FeS = 2 mol Fe₂O₃
7 mol O₂ = 2 mol Fe₂O₃  |  4 mol FeS = 4 mol SO₂ ... etc
Smart observation
You only need the two ratios involving one reference substance. All others are derivable — if 4 mol FeS = 7 mol O₂ and 4 mol FeS = 2 mol Fe₂O₃, then both equal 4 mol FeS, so they equal each other: 7 mol O₂ = 2 mol Fe₂O₃. This is algebraic equivalence (if A=B and A=C, then B=C).

Using mole ratios as conversion factors

Each equivalence statement gives two conversion factors. Choose the one where the unit you currently have appears in the denominator so it cancels.

From "4 mol FeS = 7 mol O₂"
Factor 1: (4 mol FeS / 7 mol O₂) — use when you have O₂ and want FeS
Factor 2: (7 mol O₂ / 4 mol FeS) — use when you have FeS and want O₂
Must balance first — always
If the equation isn't balanced, the coefficients are wrong. Wrong coefficients give wrong mole ratios. Wrong mole ratios give wrong answers for every problem that follows. There are no exceptions to this rule.

Mole-to-mole problems

The simplest stoichiometry type. You start in moles and end in moles — only one conversion factor needed: the mole ratio from the balanced equation.

The setup

moles A × (coeff B / coeff A) = moles B

One step. Balance → read coefficients → set up fraction so your unit cancels → calculate.

Worked examples

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O — 3 mol C₃H₈ → ? mol O₂
Ratio
1 mol C₃H₈ = 5 mol O₂
Setup
3 mol C₃H₈ × (5 mol O₂ / 1 mol C₃H₈) = 15 mol O₂
4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ — 1 mol O₂ → ? mol Fe
Ratio
3 mol O₂ = 4 mol Fe
Setup
1 mol O₂ × (4 mol Fe / 3 mol O₂) = 1.33 mol Fe
N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ — 2.75 mol NH₃ needed → ? mol N₂ required
Ratio
2 mol NH₃ = 1 mol N₂
Setup
2.75 mol NH₃ × (1 mol N₂ / 2 mol NH₃) = 1.375 mol N₂

Mass-to-mass problems

The full stoichiometry chain. Given grams of one substance, find grams of another. Three conversion factors every time, following the molar map pathway.

The template

g A × (1 mol A / MM_A) × (coeff B / coeff A) × (MM_B / 1 mol B) = g B

Worked examples

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O — 24 g O₂ → ? g H₂O
Molar masses
O₂ = 32 g/mol  |  H₂O = 18 g/mol
Mole ratio

Section 4 · Key concepts

Common mistakes

Most errors in stoichiometry come from a small recurring set of mistakes. Knowing them in advance means you can check for them deliberately.

The seven most common errors

  • Using an unbalanced equation. Wrong coefficients → wrong mole ratios → wrong everything. Balance first, always.
  • Flipping a conversion factor. If your units don't cancel, the fraction is upside down. Find it and flip it.
  • Rounding intermediate answers. Only round the final answer. Early rounding compounds error through every step.
  • Writing numbers without units. A bare number is unverifiable. Units are the error-detection mechanism of dimensional analysis.
  • Wrong molar mass. Recheck the periodic table and your atom count. Pay attention to subscripts and parentheses.
  • Mishandling parentheses. (NO₃)₂ has 2 N and 6 O, not 2 N and 2 O. The subscript outside multiplies everything inside.
  • Confusing moles and molecules. They look similar. 1 mole ≠ 1 molecule. 1 mole contains 6.022 × 10²³ molecules.

The self-check protocol

  1. Write every unit explicitly in your dimensional analysis — no skipping in your head
  2. Cross out every unit appearing in both a numerator and denominator
  3. Verify only your desired unit survives
  4. If any unwanted unit remains, find the conversion factor containing it and invert it
The unit check is the most powerful error-detection tool you have
If your units work out correctly, your setup is almost certainly right. If they don't, something is wrong regardless of what your calculator says. Trust the units over the numbers.

Sanity checks

After getting an answer, ask: does this make physical sense? If you started with 5 grams and got 400,000 grams of product, something is wrong. Useful checks:

  • If the molar mass of product is larger than the molar mass of reactant, expect more grams of product than you started with (assuming a 1:1 mole ratio)
  • If you're asked for molecules, your answer should be a very large number (order of 10²² or 10²³ for typical gram-scale samples)
  • If you're asked for moles from a few grams, your answer should be a small number (typically much less than 1 for heavier molecules)
