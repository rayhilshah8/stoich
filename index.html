Why can't we just weigh things?

In chemistry, reactions happen between molecules — not grams. Two substances can weigh exactly the same but contain completely different numbers of molecules. This matters because reactions require molecules to meet in specific ratios.

Real example from the packet
Adding 2 teaspoons of KOH to HCl gives more mass of product (KCl) than adding 2 teaspoons of LiOH — but the same number of molecules reacts in both cases. The mass difference comes from potassium being heavier than lithium, not from a difference in how many molecules reacted.

This is why "looks can be deceiving" is a theme of the class. A scale tells you mass, but not how many molecules you have. Stoichiometry is the system chemists use to count molecules indirectly using mass.

What stoichiometry actually means

Stoichiometry is the practice of quantifying a chemical reaction — finding out how many grams, molecules, or moles of something are involved. It lets you predict how much product a reaction will produce before you even run it.

Think of it like the burger restaurant example: if you know a burger needs 2 buns, 1 patty, and 1 ketchup packet, and you have 12 buns, 4 patties, and 5 ketchup packets, you can figure out exactly how many burgers you can make. Chemistry is the same — you just can't see the "patties."

The key insight is that mass is a starting point, not an ending point. You weigh something to get started, then use math to find out what that mass actually means in terms of molecule count.

Four habits that prevent struggling
  • Always write units next to every number — never write a bare number
  • Show your work — if you make a mistake, there must be a written trail to find it
  • Solve problems the way you were taught, not your own invented method
  • Avoid shortcuts until you fully understand the underlying concept
What significant figures actually are

When you measure something, you can only be as precise as your instrument allows. Significant figures (sig figs) are the digits in a number that actually carry meaningful precision — they tell the reader how carefully something was measured.

Why it matters
If you measure a desk as "1 meter" vs "1.00 meters", those mean very different things. The first says you measured to the nearest meter. The second says you measured to the nearest centimeter. Sig figs communicate that difference.
The four counting rules
  • All non-zero digits are always significant (1–9 always count)
  • Zeros sandwiched between non-zero digits are always significant — e.g. 5004 has 4 sig figs
  • Leading zeros (zeros left of the first non-zero digit) are never significant — e.g. 0.0042 has 2 sig figs
  • Trailing zeros (zeros at the end) are significant only if a decimal point appears somewhere in the number
The trailing zero rule is the most commonly missed. 1000 has 1 sig fig. 1000. has 4. 1000.00 has 6. The decimal point is the signal.

Rounding rules for calculations

For multiplication and division: your answer has the same number of sig figs as the input with the fewest sig figs.

4.5 × 6.75 = 30.375 → rounds to 30 (limited by 4.5 which has 2 sig figs)

For addition and subtraction: your answer is limited to the least precise decimal place among all inputs — not the fewest sig figs.

23,800 − 6,866 = 16,934 → rounds to 16,900 (limited by hundreds place of 23,800)
Never round intermediate answers — only round your final result. Rounding early compounds errors through every subsequent step.

Special rounding rule: when the deciding digit is exactly 5 with nothing after it, round to the even number. So 32.65 → 32.6 (6 is even), and 79.35 → 79.4 (4 is even).

What scientific notation is and why we use it

Scientific notation is a compact way of writing very large or very small numbers. In chemistry you constantly encounter numbers like Avogadro's number (602,214,150,000,000,000,000,000) or masses of individual atoms. Writing these out is impractical and error-prone.

The format is always: a number between 1 and 10, multiplied by a power of 10.

6.022 × 10²³ means 6.022 with the decimal moved 23 places right
4.7 × 10⁻⁸ means 4.7 with the decimal moved 8 places left
Converting to and from scientific notation

To convert a large number: move the decimal left until you have a number between 1 and 10. Count how many places you moved — that's your positive exponent.

340,000,000 → 3.4 × 10⁸ (moved decimal 8 places left)

To convert a small number: move the decimal right until you have a number between 1 and 10. Count how many places — that's your negative exponent.

0.000000047 → 4.7 × 10⁻⁸ (moved decimal 8 places right)
Calculating with scientific notation

Handle the numbers and the powers of 10 separately, then recombine.

Multiplication
(4 × 10⁻²)(1.5 × 10⁹) → multiply 4 × 1.5 = 6, add exponents −2 + 9 = 7 → 6 × 10⁷
Division
(8.0 × 10³) ÷ (4.0 × 10⁻⁷) → divide 8.0 ÷ 4.0 = 2.0, subtract exponents 3 − (−7) = 10 → 2.0 × 10¹⁰
After calculating, always check that your answer has a number between 1 and 10 out front. If you get 35.6 × 10³, convert it to 3.56 × 10⁴.
Fractions, ratios, decimals, and percents

These four representations all describe the same relationship between two numbers. Understanding how to move between them is essential because stoichiometry uses all four depending on context.

Ratio vs fraction: a ratio (2:1) compares part to part, while a fraction (2/3) compares part to whole. The 6/9 example simplified to ratio 2:1 means for every 2 red socks there is 1 blue sock — the total (3) doesn't appear in the ratio itself.
Negative exponents and unit notation

A negative exponent means the unit is in the denominator. This appears constantly in chemistry with units like g/cm³ or m/s².

10 kg × m⁻² is the same as 10 kg/m²
25 m × s⁻² is the same as 25 m/s²

A slash and a negative exponent mean exactly the same thing. Your teacher may use either notation interchangeably.

What a mole actually is

A mole is simply a counting unit — like a dozen (12) or a gross (144), except the number is 6.022 × 10²³. Chemists use this specific number because it creates a convenient bridge between the microscopic world of atoms and the macroscopic world of grams you can measure in a lab.

The key equivalence
1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, or ions)

The number 6.022 × 10²³ is called Avogadro's number. It was chosen so that the atomic mass of any element in atomic mass units (amu) numerically equals its molar mass in grams. Carbon's atomic mass is 12 amu, so 1 mole of carbon atoms weighs exactly 12 grams.

Don't try to "feel" how large Avogadro's number is — it's essentially incomprehensible. Just treat it as a conversion factor, the same way you'd use 12 to convert dozens to individual items.
Three things a mole can equal
  • 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (always true for any substance)
  • 1 mole = molar mass in grams (depends on the substance — use the periodic table)
  • 1 mole = 22.4 L of gas at STP (only for gases at standard temperature and pressure)
Applied to iron (Fe) and oxygen gas (O₂)
1 mole Fe = 6.022 × 10²³ atoms Fe = 55.9 g Fe
1 mole O₂ = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules O₂ = 32 g O₂ = 22.4 L at STP
Moles and molecules are not the same word — they look similar but mean completely different things. 1 mole contains 6.022 × 10²³ molecules. Never abbreviate "molecules" as "moles" in your written work.
Atomic weight vs atomic mass vs molar mass
TermDefinitionWhere to find it
Atomic weightaverage mass of one atom of an element, averaged across all naturally occurring isotopesperiodic table

Your teacher often uses "atomic mass" for all three — that's fine for calculations. Just know the distinction exists.

How to calculate molar mass

Molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance in grams. You calculate it by looking up each element's atomic weight on the periodic table, multiplying by how many atoms of that element appear in the formula, and adding everything up.

  1. Identify every element in the formula
  2. Count how many atoms of each element there are — watch out for subscripts and parentheses
  3. Look up the atomic weight of each element on the periodic table
  4. Multiply: (number of atoms) × (atomic weight) for each element
  5. Add all results together — this is your molar mass in g/mol
Example: H₃PO₄
H: 3 atoms × 1.0 g/mol = 3.0
P: 1 atom × 31.0 g/mol = 31.0
O: 4 atoms × 16.0 g/mol = 64.0
Total = 98.0 g/mol
Handling parentheses in formulas

When a formula has parentheses with a subscript outside, everything inside the parentheses gets multiplied by that subscript. This is the most common place students make mistakes.

Example: (NH₄)₂SO₄
The subscript 2 outside the parentheses multiplies everything inside:
N: 1 × 2 = 2 atoms → 2 × 14.0 = 28.0
H: 4 × 2 = 8 atoms → 8 × 1.0 = 8.0
S: 1 atom → 1 × 32.1 = 32.1
O: 4 atoms → 4 × 16.0 = 64.0
Total = 132.1 g/mol
Common mistake: treating (NO₃)₂ as 2 nitrogen and 2 oxygen. The correct count is 2 nitrogen and 6 oxygen — the subscript 2 multiplies both the N and the O₃.
What dimensional analysis is

Dimensional analysis is a method of converting between units by multiplying by fractions called conversion factors. Each conversion factor equals exactly 1 — it's just a ratio of two equivalent quantities — so multiplying by it doesn't change the value, only the units.

The core template
Known quantity × (conversion factor 1) × (conversion factor 2) = desired quantity

Unit A × (Unit B / Unit A) × (Unit C / Unit B) = Unit C

The key is that units cancel just like numbers in fractions. If the same unit appears in both a numerator and a denominator, it cancels out. You keep doing this until only your desired unit remains.

Equivalence statements and conversion factors

An equivalence statement says two different-looking quantities are equal: 1 minute = 60 seconds. From any equivalence statement you can make two conversion factors — one for each direction of conversion.

From "1 minute = 60 seconds"
Conversion factor 1: (1 min / 60 sec) — use this to convert seconds to minutes
Conversion factor 2: (60 sec / 1 min) — use this to convert minutes to seconds

Which one you use depends entirely on which unit you want to cancel. The unit you're converting FROM must appear in the denominator of your conversion factor so it cancels out.

If you set up the conversion factor the wrong way, your units won't cancel and you'll get a nonsensical answer. Always check that the unit you started with disappears before moving on.
Multi-step dimensional analysis

Some conversions require chaining multiple conversion factors together. The unit cancellation tells you what to do at each step.

Converting 2778 miles to days of driving at 70 mph, 6 hours/day
2778 miles × (1 hr / 70 miles) × (1 day / 6 hrs) = 6.6 days

Miles cancel with miles, hours cancel with hours, leaving days.
Before calculating, write out the full chain of units and check they cancel properly. This is faster than recalculating a wrong answer.
The mole island diagram — your roadmap

Every mole conversion problem can be mapped onto the mole island diagram. Moles always sit at the center. Every other quantity — mass, particles, volume — connects to moles through a specific conversion factor. You can never skip moles and go directly from one outer quantity to another.

Converting betweenConversion factor to use
Mass ↔ Molesmolar mass (g/mol) from periodic table
Particles ↔ MolesAvogadro's number (6.022 × 10²³ particles/mol)
Volume ↔ Moles (gases only at STP)22.4 L/mol
Grams to moles

Use the molar mass as your conversion factor, with grams in the denominator so they cancel.

50 g Br₂ × (1 mol Br₂ / 160 g Br₂) = 0.31 mol Br₂

The molar mass of Br₂ is 160 g/mol because each bromine atom is 80 g/mol and there are two of them.

Moles to grams

Flip the conversion factor — put moles in the denominator and grams in the numerator.

2.4 mol S × (32 g S / 1 mol S) = 76.8 g S
Grams to molecules (two-step)

You must go through moles — you can't convert grams directly to molecules. This requires two conversion factors chained together.

3 g NaCl × (1 mol NaCl / 58.5 g NaCl) × (6.022 × 10²³ molecules / 1 mol) = 3.09 × 10²² molecules
  1. Convert grams to moles using molar mass
  2. Convert moles to molecules using Avogadro's number
Counting specific atoms within molecules

Add one more conversion factor at the end using the atom count from the formula.

1.2 g aspartame (C₁₄H₁₈N₂O₅) × (1 mol / 294 g) × (6.022×10²³ / 1 mol) × (2 atoms N / 1 molecule) = 4.9 × 10²¹ atoms N

The (2 atoms N / 1 molecule) comes from the subscript on N in the formula — there are 2 nitrogen atoms in every molecule of aspartame.

What stoichiometry lets you do

Stoichiometry lets you predict how much product a reaction will make before you run it, and figure out how much reactant you need to make a specific amount of product. Without it, you'd have to run every reaction by trial and error.

A real stoichiometry question
You have 5 grams of silver nitrate (AgNO₃). You add it to sodium carbonate. How many grams of silver carbonate (Ag₂CO₃) will form? This is unanswerable without stoichiometry — weighing the reactant alone can't tell you the product mass.

The reason you can't go directly from grams of reactant to grams of product is that different substances have different molar masses. You can only compare substances meaningfully in units of moles — then convert back to grams at the end.

The three steps — always in this order
  1. Balance the chemical equation
  2. Extract mole ratios from the coefficients of the balanced equation
  3. Use dimensional analysis with those mole ratios to convert from what you know to what you want
If you skip step 1 and use an unbalanced equation, your mole ratios will be wrong, and everything downstream will be wrong. Always balance first, no exceptions.
The molar map pathway

For any mass-to-mass stoichiometry problem, you always follow this exact path:

grams A → moles A → moles B → grams B

This requires exactly three conversion factors:

  • Molar mass of A (to go from grams A to moles A)
  • Mole ratio from the balanced equation (to go from moles A to moles B)
  • Molar mass of B (to go from moles B to grams B)
The mole ratio in the middle is what makes this chemistry rather than just unit conversion — it encodes the actual chemical relationship between the two substances.
Why equations must be balanced

The law of conservation of mass says atoms cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. A balanced equation has the same number of each type of atom on both sides of the arrow.

Unbalanced vs balanced
Unbalanced: FeS + O₂ → Fe₂O₃ + SO₂
(1 Fe on left, 2 on right — violates conservation of mass)

Balanced: 4FeS + 7O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ + 4SO₂
(4 Fe, 4 S, 14 O on each side — conserved)
How to balance an equation
  1. Write out the unbalanced equation with correct formulas for all substances
  2. Count atoms of each element on both sides — make a tally table
  3. Add coefficients (the big numbers in front of formulas) to make counts equal
  4. Start with the most complex molecule, balance metals first, then non-metals, then H and O last
  5. Recount everything to verify — both sides must match
You can only change coefficients — never change the subscripts inside a chemical formula. Changing H₂O to H₃O changes the substance entirely. Only the coefficient in front can change.
Example: Al + HCl → AlCl₃ + H₂
Tally: 1 Al, 1 H, 1 Cl on left; 1 Al, 3 Cl, 2 H on right
Need 3 Cl on left → use 3HCl → now 3 H on left
Need even H: 3 vs 2 — multiply by 2 throughout:
2Al + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂ ✓
Check: 2 Al, 6 H, 6 Cl on each side ✓
If you end up needing a fractional coefficient (like 3/2 O₂), multiply every coefficient in the equation by the denominator to get whole numbers throughout.
What a mole ratio is

A mole ratio is a conversion factor that comes from the coefficients of a balanced chemical equation. It tells you exactly how many moles of one substance relate to how many moles of another substance in that reaction.

From: 4FeS + 7O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ + 4SO₂
The coefficients 4, 7, 2, 4 tell you:
4 moles FeS reacts with 7 moles O₂
4 moles FeS produces 2 moles Fe₂O₃
4 moles FeS produces 4 moles SO₂
All other ratios can be derived from these using the same logic as equivalent fractions.
Writing mole ratios as conversion factors

Each equivalence statement from the equation gives two conversion factors — one for each direction. Choose the one that cancels the unit you currently have.

From "4 mol FeS = 7 mol O₂"
Conversion factor 1: (4 mol FeS / 7 mol O₂) — use when you have O₂ and want FeS
Conversion factor 2: (7 mol O₂ / 4 mol FeS) — use when you have FeS and want O₂
You only need to write the ratios involving your specific given and desired substances. All other ratios are derivable mathematically — if A=B and A=C, then B=C.
The full problem setup

Every stoichiometry problem follows the same structure. The only things that change are the specific molar masses and the mole ratio from the balanced equation.

  1. Identify what you're given (mass of A) and what you want (mass or moles of B)
  2. Balance the equation
  3. Calculate molar mass of A and B from the periodic table
  4. Write the mole ratio connecting A to B from the balanced equation
  5. Set up the dimensional analysis chain and verify units cancel
  6. Calculate and include units in your answer
  7. Write a verbal statement of the result
Full worked example

5 grams AgNO₃ + Na₂CO₃ → ? grams Ag₂CO₃

Step 1: balance
2AgNO₃ + Na₂CO₃ → Ag₂CO₃ + 2NaNO₃
Step 2: molar masses
AgNO₃: 108 + 14 + 48 = 170 g/mol
Ag₂CO₃: 216 + 12 + 48 = 276 g/mol
Step 3: mole ratio from balanced equation
2 mol AgNO₃ = 1 mol Ag₂CO₃
Step 4: dimensional analysis
5 g AgNO₃ × (1 mol AgNO₃ / 170 g) × (1 mol Ag₂CO₃ / 2 mol AgNO₃) × (276 g / 1 mol Ag₂CO₃) = 4.06 g Ag₂CO₃
Step 5: unit check
g AgNO₃ cancels, mol AgNO₃ cancels, mol Ag₂CO₃ cancels — leaving g Ag₂CO₃ ✓

Conversion neededEquivalence statement

moles A → moles B(coeff A) mol A = (coeff B) mol B
moles B → grams B1 mol B = (molar mass) g B
moles → molecules1 mol = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules
moles → volume (gas at STP)1 mol = 22.4 L
Mole to mole problems — the simplest type

A mole-to-mole problem gives you moles of one substance and asks for moles of another. Since you're already in moles, you only need the mole ratio — no molar mass conversions required.

moles A × (coeff B mol B / coeff A mol A) = moles B
Example: C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O — how many moles O₂ from 3 mol C₃H₈?
3 mol C₃H₈ × (5 mol O₂ / 1 mol C₃H₈) = 15 mol O₂
Balancing equations that need fractional coefficients

Some equations give you fractional coefficients as an intermediate step. Multiply everything by the denominator to restore whole numbers.

Example: Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃
Fe: need 2Fe for 1Fe₂O₃
O: need 3O, so 3/2 O₂ → 2Fe + 3/2 O₂ → Fe₂O₃ (has fraction)
Multiply everything by 2: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ ✓
Mass to mass problems — the full chain

Mass-to-mass problems are the most complete type. You're given grams of one substance and want grams of another. You need all three conversion factors chained together.

g A × (1 mol A / MM_A) × (coeff B / coeff A) × (MM_B / 1 mol B) = g B
Full worked example
H₂ + O₂ → H₂O: how many grams of water from 24 g oxygen?
Balance: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Molar masses: O₂ = 32 g/mol, H₂O = 18 g/mol
Mole ratio: 1 mol O₂ = 2 mol H₂O

24 g O₂ × (1 mol O₂ / 32 g O₂) × (2 mol H₂O / 1 mol O₂) × (18 g H₂O / 1 mol H₂O) = 27 g H₂O
Always do a sanity check — does the answer make physical sense? Water (18 g/mol) is lighter than O₂ (32 g/mol), but we get more moles of water (2 for every 1 O₂), so 27g from 24g of oxygen is plausible.
When the answer asks for molecules instead of grams

Just add one more conversion factor at the end using Avogadro's number. The rest of the setup is identical.

...× (6.022 × 10²³ molecules / 1 mol) = molecules of product
The most common mistakes in stoichiometry
  • Using an unbalanced equation — always balance before extracting mole ratios
  • Flipping a conversion factor — if your units don't cancel, the fraction is upside down
  • Rounding intermediate answers — carry full precision until the final step only
  • Forgetting to include units — a number without a unit is meaningless and unverifiable
  • Using the wrong molar mass — always recheck your periodic table values and atom counts
  • Mishandling parentheses in formulas — (NO₃)₂ has 2 N and 6 O, not 2 N and 2 O
How to self-check your dimensional analysis
  1. Write out every unit explicitly — don't skip any
  2. Cross out every unit that appears in both a numerator and denominator
  3. Check that the only unit remaining is the one you wanted
  4. If any unwanted unit survives, find the conversion factor containing it and flip it
This unit cancellation check is the single most powerful error-detection tool in the entire unit. If your units work out, your setup is almost certainly correct. If they don't, something is wrong regardless of what your calculator says.
The Flight 143 lesson

A real Air Canada plane ran out of fuel mid-flight in 1983 because ground crew used the wrong density units. They used lb/L instead of kg/L, making the fuel appear 2.2 times more plentiful than it was.

If they had written out their units and checked that they cancelled properly, the error would have been immediately obvious — multiplying liters by lb/L gives pounds, not kilograms, which should have been a red flag.

This is the real-world version of "always write your units." The habit exists precisely because errors like this are invisible without it.
