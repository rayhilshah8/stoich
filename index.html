Sections 1 & 2 — Foundations

1. Why Stoichiometry Exists

Chemistry is about counting things you cannot see. You cannot count atoms directly, so you use mass + ratios to figure out how many particles are involved.

Key idea: Mass is NOT counting. Two substances can have the same mass but different numbers of molecules.

2. The Mole (Core Concept)

1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles

Think of it like a “chemist’s dozen”—just a really huge counting unit.

Examples:

  • 1 mole of Na = 23 g
  • 1 mole of O₂ = 32 g

This connects:

  • mass ↔ moles ↔ particles

3. Dimensional Analysis (Most Important Skill)

This is how you convert units correctly using fractions.

Example:

3 yards × (36 inches / 1 yard) = 108 inches

Rule: Units must cancel

4. Metric Conversions (Base-10 System)

Each step = ×10 or ÷10

Example:

1 dg = 100 mg

Because you move 2 steps → ×100

5. Significant Figures (Precision Rules)

  • All non-zero numbers count
  • Leading zeros do NOT count
  • Trailing zeros count ONLY with a decimal

Example:

0.00450 → 3 sig figs

Operations:

  • Multiplication → least sig figs
  • Addition → least decimal places

6. Ratios (Foundation of Stoichiometry)

Ratios let you scale quantities.

Example: 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O

This means:

  • 2 moles H₂ react with 1 mole O₂

Quick Check

How many moles are in 44 g of CO₂?

Section 4 — Stoichiometry (Applications)

1. The Big Idea

Stoichiometry = using ratios + moles to predict quantities in reactions.

2. Step-by-Step Process

  1. Convert given → moles
  2. Use mole ratio
  3. Convert to desired unit

3. Example Problem

How many grams of H₂O form from 2 moles of H₂?

Balanced equation: 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O

Step 1: Ratio = 2:2 → 1:1

Step 2: 2 mol H₂ → 2 mol H₂O

Step 3: Convert to grams: 2 × 18 = 36 g

Answer: 36 g H₂O

4. Limiting Reactant

The limiting reactant is the one that runs out first.

It determines how much product you can make.

5. Why Mass Alone Fails

From your packet:

  • Same mass ≠ same number of molecules
  • You must convert to moles first

Practice

If you have 3 moles of O₂, how many moles of H₂O form?
